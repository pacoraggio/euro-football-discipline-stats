Good framing. Let me give you an honest scientific assessment of the baby step and the data structure challenge.

The Baby Step — Scientific Assessment
The core idea is sound. Using cards/fouls as a normalized rate removes the confound that aggressive teams foul more, isolating the question: given a foul was committed, how likely is it to be sanctioned? This is a legitimate proxy for referee strictness toward a team.

But the ratio has a statistical problem. yellow_cards / fouls is a proportion computed from counts. You can't treat it like a continuous variable and run t-tests or ANOVA on it — the denominator varies per match/season, so ratios from a match with 3 fouls and one with 30 fouls carry very different uncertainty. The rigorous approach is:

Model yellow_cards ~ Poisson (or negative binomial) with log(fouls) as an offset and team as a factor. The offset is key — it says "we expect cards to scale proportionally with fouls; we're testing if some teams deviate from that."

For a first baby step, a simpler approximation is acceptable: compute the ratio per match, then use a Kruskal-Wallis test (non-parametric, doesn't assume normality) to compare one team vs all others. Just be aware it's an approximation.

The Data Structure Problem — This is the Real Issue
You have a fundamental granularity mismatch:

National	International
Unit	Single match	Season aggregate
Seasons	10	Multiple
Fouls available	Per match	Per season
This means you cannot directly compare the two datasets with the same statistical test. You have two options:

Option A — Aggregate national data to season level
Collapse match-level national data to per-season totals (sum fouls, sum cards per team per season). Now both datasets are on the same scale and comparable. You lose within-season variance, but the comparison becomes valid.

Option B — Keep them separate, compare conclusions
Run the analysis independently on each dataset. Then ask: does team X appear as an outlier in both? This is weaker (no formal joint test) but scientifically honest given the data mismatch — you're not forcing incompatible data into the same model.

My recommendation for a baby step: Option A first, because it lets you say something unified. Option B as a robustness check.

One More Thing to Flag Early
The cross-competition check ("does anomaly in national also appear in international?") is a good idea, but not all teams play in both. The subset of teams in international tournaments is not random — they are the strongest teams in their leagues. This selection bias means you cannot generalize: finding that top clubs behave differently in Europe vs domestically is confounded by the fact that they face stronger opponents there.

Worth acknowledging this limitation explicitly when you write up results.

Summary of what to decide before writing code:

Will you aggregate national data to season level, or keep match-level?
Will you use ratio + Kruskal-Wallis (simpler) or Poisson + offset (rigorous)?
Red cards should be modeled separately from yellow cards — they are much rarer and the distributions are very different.

This is a well-grounded pedagogical philosophy, and honestly more honest about how data science actually works than most textbooks or courses admit. A few thoughts:

What's particularly strong about this framing:

The three points you listed map directly onto real scientific practice:

"Draw conclusions from data you can trust" — this is the entire point of EDA, and most courses treat it as a checkbox rather than a gatekeeping step. Making it explicit is valuable.
"You might only partially fulfill your original goal" — this is the most under-taught lesson in data science. Students expect a clean answer; practitioners know you often end up answering a slightly different question than you started with.
"Start with a plan, adapt as you go" — this is the difference between exploratory and confirmatory analysis, and the tension between them is a genuine methodological topic worth addressing.
One thing to make explicit in the notebook:

The transition moments — where you discover something in the data that forces you to adapt — are the most pedagogically valuable parts. Consider marking them explicitly as they happen, e.g., a markdown cell that says something like:

"At this point we discovered X. This means we cannot do Y as originally planned. Instead, we will..."

Without that narration, a reader might not notice the adaptation happened, and the whole lesson is lost.

One honest challenge with this approach:

There is a thin line between adapting to data and p-hacking or post-hoc rationalization. A reader (or a critic) might ask: did you decide the analysis based on what the data could support, or did you keep looking until the data said something interesting?

The cleanest way to preempt this in a pedagogical context is to state your original hypothesis explicitly upfront, then show where reality diverged. That way the adaptation looks like scientific integrity, not convenient storytelling.

In short: the structure is right. The notebook as you've described it — EDA as a trust-building exercise, wrangling as a shaping exercise, followed by analysis constrained by what the data actually supports — is a more honest and useful template for teaching than the typical "load clean data, run model, get result" workflow.
